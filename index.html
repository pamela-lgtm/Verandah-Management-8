Fees & Compensation — Section 10

There are two primary fees and they work very differently:

Leasing Fee (one-time): 50% of the first month's rent, paid once when a new tenant's lease is executed. At $1,895/month, that's $947.50 — charged once per new tenancy, not annually.
Monthly Management Fee: 8% of rent collected each month. At $1,895, that's $151.60/month. This is only charged when rent is collected — we don't charge management fees on vacant months.
Lease Renewal Fee: $200 flat when your current tenant renews. No new leasing fee — just $200 to cover the administrative renewal process.

The marketing fee is waived for your first year. For any future new tenant placements, a $100 marketing fee applies.

They cover two entirely different things. The leasing fee is a one-time cost for finding, screening, and placing a qualified tenant — advertising, showings, application processing, background and credit checks, lease drafting, and move-in coordination. It's the work of getting someone into your home.

The monthly management fee is what covers everything that happens after that — rent collection, maintenance coordination, tenant communication, compliance, monthly statements, and 20+ years of experience navigating whatever comes up. You're not paying 8% for billing — you're paying 8% for full-service professional oversight of your investment every single month.

Think of it this way: the leasing fee is the setup; the management fee is the ongoing service. Both are necessary, both are reasonable, and together they are what make professional management work.

Term, Termination & Your Control — Section 2

Written notice to us — email qualifies — starts the 30-day clock. The termination is effective only when we receive it in writing, not from the date of a phone conversation.

The 30 days allows us to properly hand off tenant communications, finalize accounting, return access devices, and ensure nothing falls through the cracks for your tenant. It's a professional runway, not a penalty.

Important: If the property is currently leased when you terminate, the lease itself remains in force until its natural end date — terminating the management agreement does not terminate the tenant's lease. Florida law protects tenants' right to quiet enjoyment through the lease term.

Rental Pricing & Your Input — Section 3

You absolutely have input — and we encourage it. The agreement grants us authority to set and adjust pricing based on market conditions, but in practice we present every pricing recommendation to you with supporting CMA data and discuss it before listing.

What this clause is really designed to do is allow us to act quickly when the market signals a needed adjustment — without requiring a signature every time we respond to market conditions. Requiring owner approval on every small adjustment would create delays that cost you vacancy days.

Here's our real practice: We present you with a recommended list price before going live. If the property hasn't leased within approximately 14 days and showing feedback suggests pricing resistance, we'll call you — not email — with a specific recommendation and the data behind it. You're never in the dark.

We work on a percentage of rent collected. Our incentive is always to maximize your rent — not minimize it. We have no reason to price your property low.

The agreement states that Verandah is not responsible for taking precautionary measures or securing the property in the event of hurricanes or acts of God. Here's the plain-language explanation of why — and what actually happens:

Florida law places storm preparation responsibility on the property owner. We cannot legally enter an occupied property and remove or install hurricane shutters, move furniture, or take structural precautions without both owner authorization and coordination with the tenant. It's a liability boundary, not indifference.

What we do in practice: Before a named storm, we communicate with tenants about their responsibilities under the lease (securing their personal property, reporting damage immediately). We monitor properties post-storm and dispatch vendors for emergency repairs as quickly as possible. We keep owners updated throughout.

We strongly recommend ensuring your homeowner's insurance is current and that your policy covers rental activity — this is the primary protection mechanism for storm-related damage.

Your Money — When & How You Get Paid — Sections 5(n), 5(o), 5(p)

Funds are electronically deposited to you after tenant payments have cleared our bank. No funds are disbursed until clearance is confirmed — this protects you from bounced payments.

Disbursement order:

  • Our management fees and any reimbursements due
  • Operating expenses and vendor invoices for your property
  • Reserve replenishment (if the $500 reserve dipped below minimum)
  • Remaining balance — that's your disbursement
You'll receive a detailed monthly statement reflecting every line item — rent collected, fees deducted, repairs billed, and your net proceeds. Nothing is hidden.

The $500 owner reserve is a small operating buffer held in our trust account. It exists so that when a routine repair comes up — a broken garbage disposal, a clogged drain, a failed smoke detector — we can address it immediately without waiting for a wire transfer from you.

Timing matters in property management. A tenant who has to wait three days for a minor repair because the owner's funds weren't available is a dissatisfied tenant. The reserve eliminates that friction entirely.

If the reserve is used, we replenish it from the next rent disbursement before sending you the balance. The reserve is required before marketing begins — no property goes live until it's funded and cleared.

Note: If you default on your mortgage, the required reserve increases to $5,000 to cover potential owner-obligated repairs as required by law.

Tenants, Occupancy & Compliance — Sections 8, 11, 22

This is one of the most important boundaries in professional property management — and it exists entirely to protect you.

The legal risk: If you contact your tenant directly and a dispute later arises — about repairs, rent, anything — you may be required to testify in court. Every direct communication becomes potential evidence. The moment you're in the chain of communication, you're also in the chain of liability.
The management risk: Direct owner contact with tenants undermines our authority to enforce the lease. If a tenant knows they can bypass us and call you directly, lease enforcement becomes very difficult. It creates confusion about who is responsible for what — and that confusion is always exploited.

Our job is to be the professional buffer. If a tenant has a concern, we handle it. If we need your input, we bring it to you through the appropriate channel. Direct tenant contact by an owner is grounds for immediate termination of this agreement.

Think of it as the same reason you don't negotiate directly with the other party in a legal matter when you have an attorney — it's not distrust, it's good process.

The agreement notes that Verandah does not provide security services or routine property inspections beyond standard lease enforcement. Here's what that means in practice — because "standard lease enforcement" actually covers quite a bit:

  • Move-in inspection: Time-stamped, room-by-room photo documentation before the tenant takes possession
  • Vendor observations: Every vendor who enters the property is a set of eyes — they report anything unusual
  • Renewal walk-throughs: At lease renewal, we conduct a property condition review
  • Maintenance patterns: Unusual repair requests can signal occupancy issues or unauthorized pets
  • Annual inspections: Available as an add-on service if you want scheduled interior inspections
Unauthorized pets: Our pet screening process at move-in documents approved animals. Any new animals are a lease violation and addressed through our formal notice process. Our pet policy (Section 22) is designed so that owners who accept pets responsibly get significantly better tenant pools — while unapproved animals remain a clear lease violation.

By signing this agreement, you agree that our marketing ad will note that pets are considered. This is a deliberate and important policy — here's why it works in your favor:

Refusing all pets eliminates more than 50% of qualified renters immediately. The well-qualified tenants we work to place are responsible people who will not allow an animal to damage a home they value. Over 20+ years, pet-related damage among properly screened tenants has been minimal and consistently within normal security deposit territory.

What "will be considered" means in practice:

  • Every applicant with a pet goes through our professional pet screening service
  • Aggressive breed restrictions apply — these are not permitted regardless of screening
  • Approved pets are documented; unauthorized additions are a lease violation
  • ESAs and service animals are screened through a verifiable application process for legal compliance
Refusing pets also increases ESA requests — which you are legally required to accommodate regardless of your pet policy. Our approach handles ESA compliance properly while giving you a dramatically larger qualified applicant pool.

Additionally, your property is enrolled in the Animal Protection Guarantee — up to $1,500 in pet-related damage coverage beyond what the security deposit covers.

Selling the Property — Section 9

Under Florida law, your tenant has the right to quiet enjoyment of the property through the end of their lease. This means you cannot list the property for sale and conduct showings at any time during the lease.

What the agreement allows: You may list the property for sale during the last 60 days of the tenant's lease. Any showings or sale-related activities must be coordinated through us in compliance with lease terms and tenant rights.

The practical path most owners take: wait for the lease to end naturally, then list. This also gives you a cleaner property to show and avoids tenant friction during the sales process.

If you want to move back in yourself, the same principle applies — you may not unilaterally terminate an active lease. We can walk you through the proper process for non-renewal when the time comes.

HOA, Utilities & Miscellaneous — Sections 5(j), 7(b), 8(c), 16

All HOA fees, assessments, and fines remain your responsibility as the owner — not ours to pay from management operations. This includes recreation fees, special assessments, any fines levied by the HOA, and any delinquent fees that could impact your tenant's rights.

Critical: If HOA fees become delinquent and begin to affect your tenant's access to amenities or the property, we have the right to use rental proceeds to bring payments current. If rental proceeds are insufficient, you remain solely responsible.

You must provide us with the most current HOA rules and regulations. Tenants will be required to comply with all HOA rules as part of their lease terms — any violations are addressed as lease violations.

Tenants are required to place all utilities in their own name at move-in. We cannot activate or manage utilities on your behalf — providers require accounts in the property owner's name.

While the property is vacant: You must keep electricity and water active in your name. This is non-negotiable — we need utilities active to conduct showings, prepare the property, and handle any maintenance that arises before a tenant moves in.
Never terminate utilities on an occupied property. This is a serious Florida landlord-tenant law violation that can expose you to significant legal liability, fines, and potential claims from the tenant. If this ever happens — even accidentally — contact us immediately.

Federal law requires us to issue a 1099 form for any owner who receives $600 or more in rental income in a calendar year — which is virtually every owner. Your SSN or EIN is required for accurate tax reporting.

Your 1099 will be delivered electronically through the Owner Portal each year. You'll receive an email notification when it's available and can download it in PDF format at any time.

Verandah does not provide tax filing services — your tax obligations related to rental income are your responsibility. We strongly recommend working with a tax professional who understands rental property income, depreciation, and deductions.

Under Florida law, Verandah Properties acts as a transaction broker — providing limited representation to both owner and tenant without fiduciary duties to either party. This is standard in Florida property management.

What this means in plain language: we are not exclusively your advocate in the way an attorney would be. We are a professional intermediary whose job is to manage the property relationship fairly, lawfully, and in accordance with the lease terms. We serve your interests as the owner while fulfilling our legal obligations to tenants.

This is why the "no direct tenant contact" rule matters — it preserves our ability to function as an effective neutral professional on your behalf.
